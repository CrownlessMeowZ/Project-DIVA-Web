Game History

Fifteen years of rhythm. From a 2009 PSP experiment to a global PC phenomenon — every title in the Project DIVA series, documented.

2009 July 2, 2009
Series Debut PSP
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA (PSP)

  • The title that launched an entire genre of Vocaloid gaming — released exclusively in Japan for PlayStation Portable on July 2, 2009.
  • 36 songs at launch, including formative NicoNico Douga classics: World is Mine, Melt, Hachune Miku no Nichijou, and Levan Polkka.
  • Introduced the core note-pressing mechanic: four note types (circle, cross, square, triangle) appear on screen mapped to PSP face buttons, timed to the music.
  • Pioneered the Module (costume) system — players earned Diva Points by clearing songs and spent them to unlock alternate outfits for Miku.
  • Edit Mode debuted: a custom chart editor allowing players to create and share their own note maps, establishing a fan creation tradition that would last throughout the entire series.
  • Diva Room feature let players interact with a 3D Miku model in a decorable living space, purchasing furniture with in-game currency.
  • Sold over 500,000 copies in Japan within the first year — remarkable for a game built entirely around a synthetic vocalist with no prior gaming legacy.
  • Received an enhanced re-release — Project DIVA # (September 2009) — adding more songs and costumes at a lower price point.

🎮 Project DIVA was greenlit as an experimental spin-off during Hatsune Miku's viral peak on NicoNico Douga. SEGA's internal team had no expectation it would spawn a 15-year franchise.

2010 July 29, 2010
Full Roster PSP
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA 2nd (PSP)

  • Expanded the playable roster from Miku-only to all six Crypton Future Media Vocaloids for the first time: Miku, Rin, Len, Luka, KAITO, and MEIKO.
  • Song library grew to 41 tracks, bringing in fan favourites Just Be Friends, Senbonzakura, Servant of Evil, and Kokoro.
  • Introduced the Chance Time mechanic — a highlighted note sequence mid-song that, if hit perfectly, triggers an alternate PV segment and awards a substantial score bonus.
  • Edit Mode expanded with additional note types, timing tools, and improved NicoNico Douga upload integration for community chart sharing.
  • Diva Room grew significantly with new furniture categories, character-specific items, and a more reactive 3D character model.
  • Difficulty system refined with distinct Easy, Normal, Hard, and Extreme tiers — each chart hand-crafted rather than auto-generated.

✦ A further enhanced release — Project DIVA 2nd # (November 2010) — added DLC-sourced songs and the first module sets featuring costumes from fan-favourite composers.

2011 May 12, 2011
Arcade Born Arcade · PSP
▼

Project DIVA Arcade (AC) + Project DIVA Extend (PSP)

  • Project DIVA Arcade (May 2011) debuted in Japanese game centres — a purpose-built two-panel cabinet running on SEGA's RingEdge 2 board at 1080p60, far ahead of consumer hardware.
  • The Arcade cabinet introduced Hold notes (sustained button presses) and Slide notes (panel swipes), two mechanics that would become cornerstones of all future entries.
  • Arcade content updated live via server-side patches with new songs, modules, and seasonal events — a live-service model unprecedented in rhythm gaming at the time.
  • Networked leaderboards connected players across all cabinets nationally, establishing competitive ranking culture around the series.
  • Project DIVA Extend (PSP, November 2011) brought 47 songs to handheld including The Disappearance of Hatsune Miku, Romeo and Cinderella, and Matryoshka.
  • Extend introduced the multi-character PV system — music videos could now feature all six Vocaloids performing choreographed sequences simultaneously within a single track.
  • PSP Extend also received an enhanced release — Project DIVA Extend # — as the last entry in the PSP's enhanced-re-release tradition.

🎮 The Arcade version ran until 2021 in Japanese game centres — a 10-year live-service run that accumulated over 280 songs through regular content drops.

2012 August 30, 2012
HD Era Begins PS Vita
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA f (PS Vita)

  • The series' leap to PS Vita marked the beginning of the HD era — all PVs rebuilt from scratch for the Vita's OLED display, running at full 544p with vastly improved model quality.
  • Touch notes added: the Vita's front touch panel enabled a new Star note type requiring a screen tap or swipe alongside button inputs — the first hybrid input system in the series.
  • Introduced the Technical Zone scoring system — optional highlighted sequences rewarding unbroken precision chains with massive score multipliers.
  • 38 songs at launch with fully re-choreographed PVs, several featuring cinematic camera work not possible on the PSP's hardware.
  • AR Live Mode used the Vita's rear camera to place a 3D Miku performing in real-world environments — the series' first augmented reality feature.
  • The first Project DIVA title to receive an official Western localisation — published by SEGA in North America and Europe in 2013, introducing the franchise to international audiences for the first time.
  • Western sales exceeded SEGA's projections, with the initial print run selling out in multiple markets.

✦ The lowercase "f" in the title stands for both "female" and "future" — a deliberate nod to the series' shift toward a more cinematic, character-driven identity.

2013 March 7, 2013
Home Console PS3
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA F (PS3)

  • A full HD port of the Vita game running at native 1080p on home console — the highest visual fidelity the series had achieved on a non-arcade platform.
  • DualShock 3 vibration synchronised to in-game note hits, adding tactile feedback to the rhythm experience.
  • PlayStation Move controller support added — players could use motion controllers to hit notes with physical gestures.
  • Expanded Diva Room with PS3-exclusive furniture sets and higher-resolution character models for closer inspection.
  • Cross-save functionality with the Vita version allowed progress and modules to transfer between platforms.
  • Western PS3 release (August 2013) preceded the Vita version, making it the first Project DIVA title to launch on home console in the West.

🎮 The PS3 version's commercial success convinced SEGA that Project DIVA had a sustainable home console audience — leading directly to greenlit development of Project DIVA f 2nd.

2014 March 27, 2014
Scratch Notes PS Vita · PS3
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA f 2nd (PS Vita / PS3)

  • 40 new songs expanding on the previous game's library, with deeper representation of Rin, Len, Luka, KAITO, and MEIKO as lead vocalists.
  • Introduced the Scratch note type — a directional swipe on the Vita touch panel, or analogue stick flick on controller, adding a new physical dimension to chart design.
  • Scratch notes were integrated into existing Arcade-sourced charts with bespoke redesigns — no auto-conversion.
  • PV Director mode expanded to allow players to edit camera angle, zoom, and character staging within any song's music video, then save and share custom cuts.
  • PlayStation 4 remote-play support added at launch, making f 2nd the first DIVA game playable via PS4-to-Vita streaming.
  • Western release (August 2014) was the fastest East-to-West turnaround in series history at the time — only a 5-month gap.

✦ Project DIVA f 2nd's Scratch note system was later adopted into the Arcade version as a permanent mechanic, retroactively applied to new charts added to the Arcade in 2015.

2016 March 24, 2016
Story Mode PS Vita · PS4
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA X (PS Vita / PS4)

  • A structural departure — Project DIVA X introduced a full narrative campaign called Live Quest, where songs are performed to restore energy to five music clouds representing distinct genre archetypes.
  • Five cloud categories: Cool, Cute, Elegant, Quirky, and Mysterious — each with a dedicated visual aesthetic and associated module sets valid for performance.
  • 32 songs at launch, with each track locked to a specific cloud type, requiring players to manage their module collection strategically to clear each stage.
  • The Medley system allowed chaining multiple songs in a single session — score multipliers compounded across each chain, rewarding consistent performance.
  • Gift system introduced: earning items and presenting them to characters unlocked new voiced dialogue lines and strengthened Diva Room relationships.
  • PS4 version (August 2016) ran at 1080p with DualShock 4 lightbar synchronised to in-game note types — cyan, pink, gold, and white corresponding to the four button categories.
  • Western simultaneous release: Vita version August 2016, PS4 version November 2016 — the most globally synchronised launch in the series.

✦ Project DIVA X was the final mainline entry for the PSP/Vita generation, closing seven years of handheld DIVA. The series would not return to portable hardware until the Nintendo Switch.

2017 January 24, 2017
220+ Songs PS4
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA Future Tone (PS4)

  • A landmark release — the first time the complete Project DIVA Arcade library was brought to a home console, delivering over 220 songs at launch with full arcade-faithful charts.
  • Split into two separately purchasable packs: Colorful Tone (pop, ballad, and soft tracks) and Future Sound (electronic, uptempo, and extreme-difficulty tracks) — plus a combined full edition.
  • Native 1080p60fps on base PS4, exactly matching the arcade hardware's output quality — note charts were direct ports of arcade data, preserving precise timing windows down to the frame.
  • All Scratch, Hold, Slide, and Star note types from the Arcade version fully implemented with controller-to-panel input mapping.
  • Future Tone DX update (January 2018) added 30+ new songs, introduced the Extreme Extra difficulty tier for the most demanding players, and added PS4 Pro 4K output support.
  • Over 230 modules available — the largest costume collection in any single Project DIVA release at the time.
  • Western release (March 2017) marked the series' first PS4 debut outside Japan, receiving strong critical reception — the highest Metacritic scores in the franchise's history.

🎮 Future Tone's two-pack structure was a deliberate design decision — SEGA wanted players who only enjoyed specific sub-genres of Vocaloid music to be able to purchase only what they wanted.

2020 February 13, 2020
Nintendo Debut Switch
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA MegaMix (Nintendo Switch)

  • The first Project DIVA title on a Nintendo platform — a historic milestone bringing the series to an audience with little prior exposure to Vocaloid rhythm games.
  • MegaMix Mode debuted: a redesigned hybrid control scheme blending Joy-Con button presses with touch screen taps and analogue stick gestures, uniquely exploiting the Switch's hardware design.
  • 101 songs at launch — a curated selection drawing from the Arcade back catalogue, weighted toward fan-favourite tracks likely to resonate with new players.
  • Both MegaMix Mode and Arcade Mix Mode (traditional button-only) available for every song, letting players choose their preferred input style per track.
  • Two-player co-op introduced for the first time — both players share the screen simultaneously, each responsible for their own colour-coded subset of notes.
  • Handheld mode enabled touchscreen note inputs matching the Vita experience; TV mode maintained full button-only play with Joy-Con or Pro Controller.
  • Post-launch DLC expanded the library progressively to over 170 songs by the time MegaMix+ was announced.
  • Western release (June 2020) sold over 1 million copies globally — the best-selling single entry in series history outside Japan.

✦ MegaMix's million-seller status directly triggered SEGA's decision to develop MegaMix+ for PC — proving international demand for a full Vocaloid rhythm game on mainstream platforms.

2022 May 26, 2022
PC Debut 238 Songs
▼

Hatsune Miku: Project DIVA MegaMix+ (PC / Steam)

  • The definitive edition — MegaMix+ launched on Steam with 238 songs, combining the full Switch MegaMix library with the complete Future Tone arcade catalogue in a single title.
  • Both Arcade Mix and MegaMix Mode available for all 238 songs — the first time the complete arcade library was playable in MegaMix Mode.
  • Full 4K resolution, uncapped frame rate, ultrawide monitor support (21:9 and 32:9), and HDR output — the highest technical specification of any entry in the series.
  • Input support: keyboard, all major controllers (Xbox, DualSense, DualShock 4), and mouse — the widest compatibility of any DIVA title.
  • DualSense adaptive trigger support delivering distinct resistance per note type on PlayStation 5 controllers.
  • Steam Workshop integration enabled community-made note charts to be published and subscribed to directly — reviving the spirit of the original PSP Edit Mode at global scale.
  • 300+ modules at launch — the largest collection in any single release — with additional DLC modules throughout 2022–2023.

Post-Launch Updates: v1.0 → v1.5 (2022–2023)

  • v1.1 (June 2022) — Resolved audio desync at 96kHz+ system sample rates; AMD GPU shader precompile time reduced by ~35%; crash fix for rapid module switching.
  • v1.2 (September 2022) — 10 new DLC songs added; ultrawide UI scaling improved; full custom keybind remapping for all input types.
  • v1.3 (December 2022) — Steam Deck Verified certification achieved; DualSense adaptive trigger implementation; 5 new modules added; frame pacing improved on variable-refresh displays.
  • v1.4 (June 2023) — 5 additional DLC songs; background DLC preloader to eliminate stutter on first play; optimised high-density crowd scene rendering.
  • v1.5 (December 2023) — Final major update; accessibility additions including adjustable note size scaling, high-contrast note overlay mode, and colourblind-friendly note type indicators. Total song count reached 253.

🎮 MegaMix+ represents the culmination of fifteen years of Project DIVA development — the most complete, most technically accomplished, and most accessible entry in the series to date. It stands as the definitive platform for the franchise.
